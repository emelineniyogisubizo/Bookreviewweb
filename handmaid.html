About The Book

The Handmaid's Tale by Margaret Atwood is a story about a woman named Offred in a scary future where a strict religious group called Gilead rules America. Women have no freedom, and Offred is a Handmaid, forced to have babies for a powerful man, the Commander, and his wife, Serena Joy. She lives under tight control, always watched, and remembers her old life with her husband and daughter, who were taken away. Offred carefully talks to other Handmaids and gets close to Nick, the Commander’s driver, which is dangerous. She also learns about a secret group fighting Gilead. When her actions are found out, she’s taken away, and we don’t know if she’s saved or in trouble. The story, told by Offred, shows her struggle to stay strong in a cruel world.

The Handmaid's Tale Themes

A DEEP DIVE INTO THE STORY'S MOST SPECTACULAR THEMES

PATRIACHY

A DEEP DIVE INTO SOCIETY'S ISSUES

APRIL 25,2025

Atomic Group

Patriarchy is a social system built upon unequal power relations that men maintain over women. In “The Handmaid’s Tale”, patriarchy is a theme that illustrates Gilead’s social structure, the oppressive control men exert over women, and how it devalues women of their reproductive roles, individuality, and basic rights. Men held all political, social, and economic power, while women were categorized by their roles, eg, wives, marthas, and handmaids, each with a specific role. The handmaids, for instance, were reduced to their ability to bear children and were seen as instruments of procreation, while the wives were valued for their obedience to their husbands and the ability to run their households. The novel also highlights religious patriarchy in the sense that roles for men and women were shaped around biblical ideas of gender. For instance, in the bible where a slave or servant would perform the role of a concubine, with the resulting children being considered as part of another woman. In Gilead, women are stripped of their economic, political, and reproductive rights which aligns to the situations in our societies where women face discrimination in employment, limited access to education and restrictions in their bodily rights for example, child marriages, forced marriages and lack of access to abortion services are real life manifestations of control over women’s lives.

FEAR

A DEEP DIVE INTO SOCIETY'S ISSUES

APRIL 25,2025

Atomic Group

In the book ‘The Handmaid’s Tale’, the concept of fear is exposed in many occasions amongst the young women, who were only for the role of surrogacy, within the oppressive society of Gilead. Today, even in the contrast of societal settings between Gilead and current society, the concept of fear is still portrayed in many aspects. The Gilead setting was that, in any case of deviation from expected moral conduct, such as trying to escape from designated bounds and committing adultery, heavy punishments, which are:breaking of legs and being hung publicly respectively for the above cases, were executed on the victims, not only to stop them from messing up again but also to send out a warning to the rest, who seemed to be lucky not to be in that situation for the moment. Current society reveals fear in almost similar circumstances of unfair treatment yet choosing to move on in silence. For example the fear to lose one’s job because of complaints to unfair work conditions, the fear of imprisonment because of criticism to prevailing authorities and this can even include death cases and cases of abduction, fear of religious leaders who tend to threaten their audiences with disastrous experiences for violation of any instructions, among other fears.

POWER

A DEEP DIVE INTO SOCIETY'S ISSUES

APRIL 25,2025

Atomic Group

In the handmaid's tale, power is the main issue. Gilead’s leaders control everyone, especially women. The government, run mostly by men, takes away people's freedom to keep itself incharge. Women like Offred, a Handmaid are treated like objects, given names like “Of Fred” to show they belong to their commanders. They can’t make their own choices, and harsh punishments, like public hangings, scare them into obeying. Gilead also uses spies, called the Eyes, who watch everyone’s every move, making people afraid to speak out or act against the rules. But not everyone gives in. Offred resists quietly by holding onto her memories and telling her story, keeping her thoughts free even when her life isn’t. Another character, Moira, more openly by escaping, showing that even in a powerful system, people can find ways to push back. The book shows both how power traps people with fear and control, but also how courage, whether quiet or loud, can push back against it. The way power works in the book feels a lot like stuff going on in 2025. In Gilead, men control women’s lives, and today, some places have laws that limit what women can do, like controlling their healthcare choices. It’s not as extreme, but it’s the same idea of taking away freedom. In the book, the Eyes spy on everyone, and now, technology like phones and cameras tracks what we do every day, making it feel like someone’s always watching. Gilead’s leaders use religion to justify their power, and in 2025, some leaders push religious beliefs to make strict rules, saying it’s for everyone’s good when it’s really about control. But just like Offred and Moira, people today don’t just accept it. They resist by speaking out or protesting, fighting to keep their rights and voices, much like Offred’s storytelling or Moira’s escape.

In “The Handmaid's Tale”, religion is one of the main means of control used by Gilead. Where the society was heavily influenced by interpretation of the bible. Gilead claims to promote christianity values such as charity and forgiveness, while in reality these values are distorted and used as a way to promote the oppression of women. In the book, people in power use certain passages from the bible like the phrase “Blessed be the fruit” which is used as a greeting among handmaids, underscores their sole purpose of reproduction, reducing their identity to their biological function. This is also still a big issue in today’s society, where because of religion women are manipulated to believe they are only meant for some things not the other, mainly in African societies like Rwanda specifically where some churches prohibit women from doing certain roles, or having certain rights like how the bible says women should be submissive. This is used in today’s world to manipulate women into believing they are meant to be just house wives who respect and do whatever men say in the form of being respectful to men no matter how badly they are treated.

LOSS OF AGENCY

A DEEP DIVE INTO SOCIETY'S ISSUES

APRIL 25,2025

Atomic Group

In The Handmaid’s Tale the concept of loss of agency refers to the systematic stripping away of individual autonomy and freedom, particularly women within the oppressive society of Gilead. Gilead’s regime controls women’s bodies, identities, and decision-making ability by reducing them to vessels for procreation and instruments of state control. Control of the Body: Women are categorized as the handmaids(Tasked with reproduction only), the Marthas(tasked with doing the house chores), and the Elites (wives to the commanders). The handmaids are assigned to reproduce for the Elites, stripped of their personal freedom and forced to engage in sexual acts for procreation. The Marthas, Aunts and Wives are subjected to varying degrees of control and restriction, lacking autonomy in their lives. This shows the complete absence of reproductive rights for women in Gilead, who are not allowed to make decisions about their own bodies about their own bodies or future pregnancies. Suppression Of Identity: Gilead systematically eliminates individual names and identities, replacing them with assigned roles that reinforce the societal hierarchy. Women are denied access to education,employment, and personal property further limiting their capacity for independent thought and action. Gilead’s color-coded attire and strict rules regarding appearance further emphasize the suppression of individual expression and identity. Limited Decision-Making power: The women are novice in political decisions or the legal system. Even seemingly mundane aspects of daily life, like shopping or travel, are controlled by others. The threat of severe punishment for any deviation from the prescribed rules reinforces the lack of agency and fosters a climate of fear and compliance. The Illusion of Agency: While women in Gilead are largely powerless, the novel also explores subtle forms of resistance such as maintaining personal memories, relationships or acts of defiance. The novel’s narration by Offred highlights the importance of language as a means of resistance and maintaining a sense of self. Even in the face of immense oppression, the characters hold on to a glimmer of hope for escape and a future where they can exercise their agency.
