About the book

"Born a Crime" by Trevor Noah is a memoir that
recounts Noah's childhood and early adulthood
in South Africa, particularly during and after
apartheid. The book explores themes of identity,
race, culture, and the lasting impact of apartheid
on individuals and society. Noah, born to a Black
Xhosa mother and a white Swiss-German father,
navigates a world where their relationship was

Poverty and inequality

Noah's upbringing in marginalized
communities reveals the cyclical
nature of poverty and its impact on
individuals and communities . System
oppression can lead to limited opportunities
and perpetuation of poverty, echoing ongoing
struggles faced by vulnerable populations in various
parts of the world.

Identity, Belonging, and Community

Trevor Noah's Born a Crime dives into the
struggle to find identity and belonging in a
divided South Africa. In Chapter 15, Trevor's
shoplifting with friends highlights this. As a mixed-race
kid, apartheid's strict race categories -black, white,
coloured-leave him with no clear place. Society's old
traditions also press hard: women like his mom, Patricia,
are supposed to follow men like Abel, even though she pays
the bills. Apartheid's rules on jobs and homes make things
tougher, keeping black families trapped with little money. Trevor's
shoplifting group becomes his community, welcoming him despite his
“crime” of being mixed. They share dangers and gains, like the “Black tax”.
In my community although kids get labeled as rich, poor, and geek but groups like
teams build connections. Around the world, mixed-race people in the U.S. also face
the “where do you belong?” question. Trevor's crew proves that the community can
overcome being left out.

Apartheid's Lasting Barriers

Apartheid's impact still hangs over Born a Crime,
affecting everyone's personal and social fights. For
example, Trevor's bus driver in Chapter 17 drives without
a license to make money, knowing he could get arrested because
apartheid's rules blocked black people from good jobs. These rules
kept black South Africans stuck in townships, and even after apartheid
was gone, chances to get ahead were few. Old-school expectations made things
tougher-women like Patricia were supposed to focus on family, often helping men
like Abel, whose garage didn't work out. Apartheid's separation split communities,
leaving them clueless about each other's lives. Still, Trevor's hard work builds a
group with other drivers, sharing ways to avoid cops, a bit like the “Black tax,”
where those doing well help those who aren't. In my town, schools in minority areas
get less money, showing this gap, and in the U.S., housing differences are similar.
Trevor's story proves that communities give a sense of belonging despite systems
that push people out.

Education And Social Mobility

Noah's journey highlights the struggles of
access to education and social mobility faced
by many South Africans, particularly those
from marginalized communities.

Humor & Resilience

Noah uses humor and wit to navigate
the difficult situations and experiences
of his childhood, showcasing his resilience
and ability to find joy in the face of adversity.
